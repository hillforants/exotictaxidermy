# Data Management Plan

**Project Overview**

Our theme for our collection is exotic taxidermy from the Victorian Era (1837-1901). For this project, we are using CollectionBuilder, GitHub, and Google Sheets. These technologies allow us to present an interactive medium to examine the impacts of Orientalism and exoticism on high-end Victorian tastes. Our project functions as a conglomerate of exotic taxidermy specimens, and how different animals are chosen to be represented in Victorian displays. The intended audience of this collection is anyone curious about the history of taxidermy or researchers focused on Victorian furniture preference and collection habits. This collection sheds light on the morbid technology of taxidermy during this time period, and how taxidermist pioneers created the taxidermist culture that our modern advancements stem from today. 

**Roles and Responsibilities**

The creators of this project are students Harley Antflick and Olivia Heffernan. We were able to split our responsibilities proactively, making sure that we both had a hefty hand in the production of this collection. Antflick contributed in the creation and customization of the Github coding, and the introduction of themes into the website. Heffernan contributed to the informative metadata present in the website, and how pieces are presented in our collection. Together, we created a solid collection of works that represented both of our efforts toward Victorian taxidermy exposure.

**Data Inventory and Provenance**

For this project, we used various sources from notable taxidermist research websites, as well as accredited research facilities and archives with historical imagery of the preparation of taxidermy, and the displaying of taxidermy, including the Smithsonian Institute and the Digital Archive of America.

**Storage and Stewardship**

As the term continues, our project lives in the public realm, accessible by anyone who is interested. Following the term, the project will remain in the public realm, as we would like anyone to be able to access the information after we have finished this collection. The files for this project are stored in Google Drive, GitHub, and Google Sheets. These files are able to be accessed and edited by creators Olivia Heffernan and Harley Antflick, but the CollectionBuilder website and GitHub repository are able to be viewed by the public. Maintaining this project would mean adding additional imagery, upkeep of information if information is updated or revealed, as well as edits to code in the event that code shifts over time. This project will be maintained for the purpose of this class, and will not be edited after the termination of this term. If GitHub Pages stops serving the site and repository, the site will remain terminated. 

**Access, Reuse, and Rights**

The public may use our repository and CollectionBuilder has references in the future under certain circumstances. In order to utilize our collection, the individual or group must credit the original creators (Harley Antflick and Olivia Heffernan), as well as properly citing the resource that they use to the original source. If an individual or group were to use imagery sourced from the Smithsonian Institute, we would like to be credited, along with the proper citations for the Smithsonian Institute. All imagery utilized for this collection have been cited in CollectionBuilder and do not include strict usage policies, other than general citation. Individuals and groups should consider the ethical nature of taxidermy, especially exotic taxidermy from this time period. In many instances, animal abuse may be present in the process of creation, and could offend certain audiences that may be sensitive to posthumous animal imagery. Individuals or groups should warn audiences of the nature of the imagery, and that the animals showcased in the collection are deceased. For safeguarding purposes, a warning about this content is included in the “about” section of the GitHub page, as well as in the front-page description on the CollectionBuilder site.

One of our sources, TaxidermyHallofFame.org, has an active copyright on their webpage (as of June 8th, 2026). Our fair use argument is as follows.

Purpose and Character: Our use of photos collected from this website is purely for educational and transformative uses. We do not aim purely to document and show the works of particular taxidermists, as the Taxidermy Hall of Fame does. Our placement of these photos amongst others is to make a statement about a particular era in the history of taxidermy that was focused on exoticism.

Nature of the Work: The photos we used from the Taxidermy Hall of Fame were of works by William Temple Hornaday and John Rowley. Both taxidermists' work predate the 20th century and are highly published and recorded.

Amount and Substansiality: We sourced two photos of the hundreds available on the website. There is no feasible way to take these two photos and recreate the “heart” of any work on the website.

Effect on the Market: The Taxidermy Hall of Fame is marketed heavily toward historic preservation, as well as modern taxidermists. Neither of those audiences aligns with our collection.

Another one of our sources, Taxidermy4cash.com, contains a rights statement as follows, “All image use or reproduction by any means from this site is expressly forbidden. Subsequently, the British Historical Taxidermy Society (BHTS), who incidentally pride themselves on their 'integrity' [sic], started taking images from this site and watermarking them as their own. Maybe not the best example on Internet etiquette. Because of this poor behaviour, from now on all images on this site are copyright of the people who provided them un-water nmarked and can only be used with their express permission in their original form”. While explicit, we do feel as though we have a substantial fair rights argument.	

Purpose and Character: We are using these images for educational and transformative purposes. By showing off the elaborate staging of some Victorian taxidermy, we further our critique of the broader Victorian fascination with the “other”. Additionally, it seems like this statement of copyright was written with a specific instance in mind, one that our circumstances have no relation to.

Nature of the work: These photos are not stylized or artistic in any traditional sense. They are documentation of taxidermy found in museums and taxidermy shops. This website is for collectors.

Amount and Substantiality: We used five photos out of hundreds featured; many more were intended to be linked but the URLs are now dead. We do not feel that this small collection could replace or capture any aspect of the original.

Effect on the Market: Taxidermy4cash.com is for collectors to inquire about pieces or see one another’s. Our collection’s audience is not expected to be an active participant in the world of modern taxidermy.
